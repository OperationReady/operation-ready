Module 2.3
The rescue boat operation

The RHIB — Rigid Hull Inflatable Boat — is the primary rescue platform used by humanitarian SAR vessels. Understanding how RHIBs operate, what happens during a launch and recovery, and what is expected of deck crew during these operations is essential for anyone working on the stern deck.

Most of the dangerous moments in a SAR operation happen during RHIB launch and recovery. Moving heavy equipment, open deck in sea state, lines under tension, and time pressure — these all combine to create a high-risk environment. Understanding the operation helps you play your part safely.

RHIB operations are restricted
RHIB operation — driving, crewing, and recovery — is carried out by trained SAR technicians. This module explains what happens during these operations so you understand and can support them safely. You will not be operating a RHIB without specific training.

Section 1 of 3
The RHIB

A RHIB (Rigid Hull Inflatable Boat) combines a solid fibreglass hull with inflatable collar tubes. This gives it stability, buoyancy, and impact resistance — qualities essential for approaching distress vessels and recovering people from the water in difficult conditions.

This section is an overview. Every vessel and organisation will have significant variation in their SOPs — depending on the davits, cranes, position of the RHIBs, and the mother vessel's design. The principles below apply broadly; your vessel's induction will give you the specifics.

PPE is essential
RHIB operations are one of the highest-risk activities aboard. PPE — lifejacket, helmet, foul-weather gear, non-slip footwear, gloves, knife, head torch where appropriate — is non-negotiable for everyone in the operating area.
Why RHIBs for SAR

Fast, manoeuvrable, and stable. Can approach a distress vessel from multiple angles. The low freeboard allows the crew to communicate at eye level with people in distress and to manage transfers efficiently.

Limitations

Limited range and fuel. Sea state dependent — at higher Beaufort levels RHIB operations become progressively more dangerous. Exposed crew. Capacity is limited; multiple trips are needed for any significant number of people.

RHIB crew roles
RHIB lead
Boat leader / STL
Commands the RHIB during operations. On the lead RHIB, this is typically the STL. Responsible for the safety of the RHIB and all persons aboard. Makes approach decisions, directs the recovery, and maintains communication with the mother vessel.
Driver
Driver
Drives the RHIB under direction of the boat leader. Manages engine and fuel systems. Maintains position during the operation alongside the distress vessel.
SAR Crew
RHIB Crew
SAR Team member working in the RHIB. Carries out the physical tasks of the operation — passing lifejackets, managing lines, supporting people transferring out of the distress vessel. Works under direction of the boat leader. Cultural mediators are typically also embarked on the lead RHIB.
Deck leader
Deck leader (mother vessel)
Manages the deck during RHIB operations — coordinating launch and recovery, managing lines, directing deck crew, and communicating with the bridge. The deck leader's instructions take priority on the deck during operations.
Section 2 of 3
Launch and recovery

RHIB launch and recovery are the most operationally demanding — and most hazardous — parts of a rescue. They involve heavy equipment moving in a confined space, lines under tension, and deck crew in close proximity to the water. Understanding the sequence helps you follow instructions correctly and avoid the most common hazards.

Survivors are not recovered with the RHIB
An important point on terminology: the RHIB ferries people from the distress vessel to the mother vessel, but the RHIB itself is not lifted out of the water with survivors aboard. Survivors disembark from the RHIB at water level — typically via a boat landing, a pilot ladder, or directly onto the deck depending on the vessel — and only then is the empty RHIB recovered. The recovery sequence below describes the empty RHIB returning to its cradle.
Launch sequence (typical)
RHIB moved from cradle to embarkation zone
The RHIB starts in its cradle (storage position). It is moved or lowered to the embarkation level — the height where crew can step into it safely.
RHIB Crew embark
The RHIB Crew step in at the embarkation level, in full PPE, and confirm ready. Tag lines are attached at this level — they help control the boat's swing during the lower. The painter line is already attached.
Lower to water and release
The RHIB is lowered to the water surface. Tag lines are released as the RHIB reaches water. The painter line stays attached for the moment. Engine started, hook released. RHIB confirms ready and the painter line is then released by the deck. The RHIB departs under its own power.
Recovery sequence (typical)
Survivors disembark first
Before recovery, the RHIB transfers any survivors aboard to the mother vessel — via boat landing, pilot ladder, or directly onto the deck depending on the vessel. The RHIB only returns for recovery once empty of survivors.
RHIB to recovery position
The empty RHIB returns to the recovery position alongside the mother vessel. The painter line is passed and attached. The hook is lowered and connected.
Lift to embarkation level
The RHIB is lifted out of the water to the embarkation level. As it reaches that level, tag lines are attached to control swing.
Crew exit and RHIB to cradle
RHIB Crew exit at embarkation level. Tag lines are released. The RHIB is then moved or lifted to its cradle for stowage and re-readiness.
Lines: painter and tag
Painter line — always attached whenever the RHIB is in the water. It is the primary connection between RHIB and mother vessel. Tag lines — attached and released only at the embarkation level, used to control the boat's swing during lifting and lowering. Tag lines are not in the water.
Critical safety moments
The highest-risk moments during launch and recovery are: hook attachment and release (pinch and crush risk), the RHIB swing during lowering and lifting (being struck by the boat or hook), and tag-line handling under tension (snap-back risk). If you are not specifically assigned to a task, stay back from the operating zone and maintain a clear path for assigned crew.

Deck crew role during RHIB operations

During RHIB operations, non-SAR deck crew have specific supporting roles. Understanding what is expected — and what is not — helps operations run safely and efficiently.

Stay back unless assigned

The deck during a RHIB operation is a controlled space. Unassigned crew on deck create hazards — they obstruct movement, create confusion in a fast-moving operation, and put themselves at risk. If you are not specifically assigned a task, stay back from the operating zone.

Follow the deck leader's instructions — and use the clarification loop

During operations, instructions from the deck leader are followed immediately. If you are unsure of an instruction — what was said, where to go, what to do — say so right away. A short clarification loop ("confirm — you want me to take the line and step back?") is the right response, not silence followed by guessing. Operations move fast; honest clarification is faster than mistakes.

Survivor reception readiness

When the RHIB returns with survivors, assigned crew are ready at the boat landing or transfer point to assist people aboard. Be in position early — not running over when the RHIB is already arriving. Your team leader will brief your specific role in advance.

Maintaining communication

During RHIB operations, radio communications between the RHIB, deck, and bridge are continuous. Deck crew with radios maintain awareness of the operation's status. Keep radio channels clear of non-essential communication during a RHIB operation.

Preparing during transit
When your vessel is in transit to a rescue position, the SAR team is preparing — checking RHIB systems, fuelling, checking PPE, confirming crew roles. As a general crew member, you prepare too — rest if you can, eat something, put on appropriate PPE, and be ready at your assigned station when the operation begins. Preparation during transit is part of the operation.
