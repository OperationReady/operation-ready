Module 6.2
The legal architecture of maritime SAR

The SAR system is not just an operational framework — it is a legal structure. States have binding obligations to maintain SAR services, coordinate responses within their Search and Rescue Regions, and designate places of safety for rescued persons. Understanding these obligations — who holds them, what they require, and where they are frequently unmet — contextualises the operational environment humanitarian NGOs work in.

Section 1 of 2
State obligations and the SAR system

The SAR Convention requires states party to the convention to ensure adequate SAR services are maintained within their Search and Rescue Regions. This is a binding obligation — not a best-effort standard.

SAR Convention — state obligations
What states must provide
States must: establish, staff, and equip MRCCs; coordinate responses to distress events within their SRR; cooperate with adjacent states on SAR; and ensure that rescued persons are delivered to a place of safety. The obligation to ensure SAR coverage does not diminish in proportion to the volume of maritime traffic or the political sensitivity of those in distress.
MRCC responsibilities
Coordination centres and their legal role
Maritime Rescue Coordination Centres have formal legal responsibility for coordinating SAR within their assigned SRR. This includes receiving distress alerts, assigning assets, coordinating the response, and designating a place of safety for rescued persons. MRCC decisions carry legal weight — they are not suggestions. When your vessel operates as OSC at MRCC direction, it is operating within a legally defined coordinating structure.
The place of safety obligation
A legal requirement, not a favour
Once a rescue is complete, the MRCC responsible for the SRR where the rescue occurred has the legal obligation to designate a place of safety. A rescue vessel cannot fulfil its obligations simply by pulling people from the water — the legal duty of rescue extends to delivery to a place of safety. Delays in POS designation — which are well-documented in the Central Mediterranean — do not transfer the obligation to the rescue vessel, but they do create protracted situations with significant humanitarian consequences.
Where the system functions — and where it does not
The SAR system functions well in contexts where states have adequate resources, political will to respond, and consistent cooperation between adjacent MRCCs. In the Central Mediterranean, the combination of high distress volumes, contested SRR boundaries, political disputes about disembarkation, and the deterioration of Libyan SAR capability has created a persistent gap between legal obligation and operational reality. NGO vessels exist to fill part of this gap — within the legal framework, not outside it.
Section 2 of 2
NGO vessels — legal standing and obligations

Humanitarian NGO rescue vessels are not legally separate from the SAR system. They carry the same legal obligations as any other vessel and operate within the same framework. This has sometimes been characterised as controversial, but the legal position is clear.

The same duties apply
SOLAS and UNCLOS bind every vessel
The duty to render assistance under SOLAS Chapter V and UNCLOS Article 98 applies to every vessel on the sea, regardless of flag or purpose. A humanitarian NGO vessel carries this duty no differently from a commercial cargo vessel or a state navy vessel. Operating systematically in areas where people are frequently in distress, and responding to those distress events, is the fulfilment of this legal obligation — not a political act.
Integration with the MRCC
NGO vessels coordinate within the system
NGO vessels are required to communicate with the relevant MRCC before, during, and after rescue operations. They provide position reports, rescue notifications, casualty information, and requests for POS designation. This communication is both a legal requirement and an operational one. An NGO vessel that operates without MRCC coordination is not operating legally — and creates safety and coordination risks for everyone in the area.
OSC designation
Formal coordination roles and their weight
When an NGO vessel is designated as On-Scene Coordinator by an MRCC, it assumes formal legal responsibility for coordinating the rescue at the scene. This is not an informal role — it carries responsibility for the overall management of the rescue and for communication back to the MRCC. Understanding this when your vessel receives OSC designation matters for how you operate and document the response.
Legal questions go to leadership
The legal complexities of operating in the Central Mediterranean — contested jurisdictions, disputed SRR boundaries, MRCC non-response, POS delays — are managed by your organisation's leadership, legal advisors, and SARCO. These are not problems for individual crew members to navigate. Your role is operational. When a situation with legal implications arises, refer to the SARCO or Head of Mission.
